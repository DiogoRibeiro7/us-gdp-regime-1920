# Article outline

## Working title

**The United States did not grow at one speed: GDP regimes from 1920 onward**

## Thesis

From 1920 onward, United States real GDP growth is better described as a sequence of regimes than as a single stable process. A long-run trend regression captures the average path, but piecewise regression on annual growth reveals periods of structurally higher and lower growth.

## Structure

### 1. Introduction

Open with the problem of treating GDP growth as if it were a single long-run average. Explain that averages hide regime changes.

### 2. Data

Explain why the project uses Maddison for the 1920-onward historical window and FRED/BEA for validation from 1929 onward.

### 3. Method

Present two models:

- log real GDP trend regression,
- piecewise constant regression on annual real GDP growth.

Explain above/below-mean labels.

### 4. Results

Include:

- log real GDP trend figure,
- GDP growth regime figure,
- regime table,
- comparison with FRED/BEA in overlapping years.

### 5. Interpretation

Map statistical regimes to historical periods, but avoid claiming causality from the model alone.

### 6. Robustness

Show whether regimes survive alternative penalties, segment sizes, and source comparisons.

### 7. Conclusion

Conclude that GDP growth history is regime-dependent. The mean is useful, but insufficient.

## Required figures

1. Log real GDP and fitted trend.
2. Annual growth with long-run mean and shaded regimes.
3. Optional: Maddison versus FRED growth comparison from 1929 onward.
4. Optional: sensitivity heatmap of break years under different settings.

## Required tables

2. Trend regression summary.
