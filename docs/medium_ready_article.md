# The United States Did Not Grow at One Speed

## GDP regimes from 1920 onward

The long-run history of United States real GDP is often summarized with a single
average growth rate. That average is useful, but it hides a lot. The United
States did not grow at one constant speed after 1920. Its growth history is
better read as a sequence of regimes: periods when annual growth sat above or
below the economy's own long-run average.

The first step is to separate the long-run trend from year-to-year growth. The
trend model fits a straight line to log real GDP, which gives a compact estimate
of average exponential growth. It is a useful baseline, not a claim that the
economy followed one smooth path.

## Data and source choices

The historical series uses the Maddison Project Database 2023. Maddison provides
GDP per capita and population estimates over a long historical window, which
makes it suitable for a 1920 starting point. Total real GDP is constructed as GDP
per capita multiplied by population.

FRED/BEA `GDPCA` is used differently. It is the modern national accounts
validation source from 1929 onward. The distinction matters: Maddison is the
long-run historical source, while FRED/BEA is the overlapping benchmark for the
modern national accounts period.

## Why regimes matter

GDP levels are not used for above-mean and below-mean labels. Levels trend
upward over long periods, so comparing levels with their full-sample average
would mostly separate early decades from later decades. Growth rates are the
right object for this article because they ask whether the economy was expanding
faster or slower than its own long-run growth average.

## Reading the regimes

The piecewise model divides annual real GDP growth into segments. Each segment
has one mean growth rate. Segments above the full-sample mean are labelled
`above_mean`; the others are labelled `below_mean`.

These labels are descriptive. A breakpoint near a major historical event does
not prove that the event caused the breakpoint. The model identifies changes in
the growth pattern. Historical interpretation still requires historical
evidence.

## Historical interpretation

The regime sequence should be read alongside the major economic periods of the
last century: the Great Depression, World War II mobilisation, postwar
adjustment, postwar expansion, the productivity slowdown after the early 1970s,
the inflation and disinflation period, the Global Financial Crisis, and the
COVID-19 shock and recovery.

The right language is cautious. A statistical regime may coincide with a known
historical period, or be consistent with a historical interpretation, but the
piecewise regression alone does not identify policy causality.

## Robustness and validation

The model is checked across different minimum segment sizes, BIC and AIC
criteria, and exclusion of unusual historical windows such as 1941-1945. Stable
break years are those that recur across these modelling choices.

When FRED/BEA data are available, overlapping annual growth rates are compared
against the Maddison-derived series. The comparison reports correlation, mean
absolute difference, root mean squared difference, and the years with the
largest differences.

## Data and code availability

The analysis is reproducible from the repository pipeline. Raw downloaded data
are not committed. Processed tables, model outputs, figures, and article assets
are generated by the code.

## Conclusion

The main lesson is not that any single event explains American growth. It is
that the average growth rate is not the whole story. From 1920 onward, United
States real GDP is more informative when read as a trend plus a sequence of
growth regimes.
