# The United States Did Not Grow at One Speed

## A reproducible look at GDP growth regimes since 1920 — and why the long-run trend line is a statistical trap

The long-run history of United States real GDP is usually summarised with a
single average growth rate. That average is convenient, but it hides most of the
interesting structure. The United States did not grow at one constant speed
after 1920. Its growth history reads better as a sequence of regimes: stretches
when annual growth sat clearly above or clearly below the economy's own long-run
average.

This article walks through a small, fully reproducible pipeline that estimates
those regimes, tests whether they are statistically real, and places them next
to fiscal structure and worker earnings. Every number below is generated by the
code in the repository; none of it is hand-typed into the prose.

## Start with the trend — then distrust it

The natural first step is to separate the long-run trend from year-to-year
growth. Fitting a straight line to log real GDP gives a compact estimate of
average exponential growth:

| Intercept | Log trend slope | R-squared | Annualised trend growth (%) |
| --- | --- | --- | --- |
| -38.968 | 0.031 | 0.987 | 3.156 |

An annualised trend of about **3.16%** with an R² of **0.987** looks like a
strong result. It is not. Log real GDP is a highly persistent series, and a
formal augmented Dickey–Fuller test cannot reject the hypothesis that it contains
a *unit root* (p ≈ 0.99), while a KPSS test rejects trend-stationarity
(p ≈ 0.04). In plain terms: the level of GDP behaves like a random walk with
drift, and fitting a straight line to a random walk *always* produces a high R².
The trend line is descriptive, not evidence of a stable deterministic path.

The annual **growth** series behaves in exactly the opposite way — ADF rejects a
unit root (p < 0.001) and KPSS does not reject stationarity. This is the formal
justification for the whole approach: growth rates, not GDP levels, are the right
object to segment into regimes. Levels trend upward mechanically, so labelling
them "above" or "below" average would mostly separate early decades from later
ones.

## The regimes

A piecewise model divides annual real GDP growth into segments, each with its own
mean. Breakpoints are chosen by dynamic programming under a Bayesian information
criterion that penalises adding segments. On the Maddison-derived series it
selects five regimes:

| Segment | Start | End | Years | Mean growth (%) | Long-run mean (%) | Regime |
| --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- | --- |
| 1 | 1921 | 1928 | 8 | 3.19 | 2.97 | above_mean |
| 2 | 1929 | 1933 | 5 | -5.78 | 2.97 | below_mean |
| 3 | 1934 | 1944 | 11 | 8.10 | 2.97 | above_mean |
| 4 | 1945 | 1949 | 5 | -1.98 | 2.97 | below_mean |
| 5 | 1950 | 2022 | 73 | 3.11 | 2.97 | above_mean |

The sequence lines up with the familiar economic history of the century: the
1920s expansion, the 1929–1933 Depression collapse, the 1934–1944
rebound-and-mobilisation surge, postwar demobilisation, and a long, comparatively
stable postwar era. But a breakpoint near a famous event does not prove the event
caused it. The model finds changes in the growth pattern; explaining them still
requires historical evidence.

## Are the breaks real — and are the dates sharp?

Two questions that most regime write-ups skip: is each break statistically
supported, and how precisely is its date pinned down?

For the first, each additional regime is tested with a sequential
supF(l+1 | l) statistic in the spirit of Bai and Perron, with p-values from a
parametric bootstrap. The tests agree with the information criterion: additional
regimes are supported up to the fifth segment, after which extra breaks earn
large p-values and add nothing. The criterion and the significance test point to
the same number of regimes — a reassurance the point estimates alone can't give.

For the second, a residual bootstrap re-segments thousands of resampled series
and reports a confidence interval for each break year. The result is a useful
dose of humility:

- The **1929** and **1934** breaks are razor-sharp (intervals of a year or two).
- The **1945** demobilisation break is moderately identified.
- The start of the long postwar regime near **1950** is barely identified at all,
  with a bootstrap interval running out toward the late 1980s.

So the Depression and mobilisation turning points are precise, but "when the
postwar regime began" is genuinely fuzzy. A break can be statistically present
and still have a poorly determined date.

## Validating the historical series

The historical series comes from the Maddison Project Database 2023 (real GDP per
capita × population). Because Maddison is not the same object as modern BEA
national accounts, the pipeline validates it against FRED/BEA `GDPCA` over the
overlapping years (1930–2022, 93 observations): the two growth series correlate
at **0.955**, with a mean absolute gap of about **0.63** percentage points. Close
enough for regime-level analysis, and honest about the fact that the two sources
are not identical.

## GDP growth is not the same as worker buying power

The most consequential extension is distributional. Indexing to a common base
year (1979), real GDP per capita rose far faster than real median weekly
earnings — aggregate output per person more than doubled while the median
earnings index rose only modestly. Real hourly compensation lands in between.

This does not mean most people became poorer every year. It means the headline
aggregate and the median-worker measure diverged substantially, and that GDP
growth alone is an incomplete proxy for broad buying power. A full welfare
account would also need hours, household composition, transfers, taxes, benefits,
asset income, and prices by income group. Alongside this, the composition of
federal receipts shifted markedly away from corporate income taxes toward
social-insurance financing over the postwar period.

## What the analysis deliberately does *not* claim

- Statistical breakpoints are **not** automatic policy causality. They are
  structure to interpret, not proof.
- "Above mean" and "below mean" are **descriptive** labels, not welfare or
  causal statements.
- Fiscal and tax variables are **endogenous** to the same shocks that move GDP,
  so the correlations and event windows are context, not identified effects.
- The tax-burden proxies are **not** a full incidence model.

## Reproducibility

The analysis is reproducible from the repository pipeline. Raw downloaded data
are not committed; processed tables, model outputs, figures, and article assets
are all generated by the code:

```bash
us-gdp-regimes run --config config/default.yaml
us-gdp-regimes make-fiscal-context --config config/default.yaml
us-gdp-regimes make-tax-effects --config config/default.yaml
us-gdp-regimes make-distributional-analysis --config config/default.yaml
us-gdp-regimes export-report-numbers --config config/default.yaml
```

The tables and statistics quoted here are emitted by `export-report-numbers`
into `data/models/report_numbers.json` and the generated LaTeX macros, so the
written narrative cannot silently drift from the model outputs.

## Methods and references

- Trend inference uses Newey–West (HAC) standard errors (Newey and West, 1987).
- Unit-root behaviour is assessed with the augmented Dickey–Fuller test (Said and
  Dickey, 1984) and the KPSS test (Kwiatkowski et al., 1992).
- Break significance follows Bai and Perron (1998, 2003); break-date uncertainty
  is bootstrapped.
- The dynamic tax-effect layer uses local projections (Jordà, 2005).
- The GDP series is from the Maddison Project Database 2023, validated against
  FRED/BEA `GDPCA`.

## Conclusion

The lesson is not that any single event explains American growth, nor that GDP is
useless. It is that the long-run average — and even the tidy trend line — hides
the most important structure. From 1920 onward, United States real GDP is far
more informative when read as a stationary growth process broken into a small
number of statistically supported regimes, and when placed next to the fiscal and
distributional facts that GDP alone leaves out.
