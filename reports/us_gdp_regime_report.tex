\begin{abstract}
This report summarizes a reproducible empirical workflow for long-run United
States real GDP growth from 1920 onward. The analysis combines a Maddison-based
real GDP proxy, FRED/BEA validation data, piecewise growth-regime detection with
formal structural-break inference, federal fiscal context, tax-regime event
dynamics, and distributional measures linking GDP per capita to real worker
earnings. The headline statistical result is that a single long-run trend fits
the log GDP level well in-sample, but the level is statistically
indistinguishable from a unit-root process, so annual growth --- which is
stationary --- is the appropriate object for regime analysis. Growth is best
read as a sequence of regimes, and a sequential bootstrap break test confirms
the number of regimes that the information criterion selects. The distributional
result is equally important: aggregate real GDP per capita rose much faster than
real median weekly earnings after the late 1970s, so GDP growth alone is an
incomplete proxy for broad buying power. Fiscal and tax variables add useful
context, but the report distinguishes descriptive timing, lagged association,
and causal identification throughout.
\end{abstract}

\section{Executive Summary}

The project began with a simple question: whether the United States grew at one
roughly constant speed after 1920, or whether the data support distinct growth
regimes. The answer is mixed in the way macroeconomic time series usually are:
the log level of real GDP has a strong long-run trend, but the annual growth
process is segmented by depression, war, demobilisation, and a long postwar
period.

The fitted log-trend model implies an annualized trend growth rate of
approximately $\TrendGrowth\%$ with an in-sample $R^2$ of $\TrendRSquared$. That
$R^2$ should not be read as evidence for a deterministic trend: augmented
Dickey-Fuller and KPSS tests both indicate that log real GDP is consistent with
a unit root, so a straight-line trend mechanically fits a persistent series
well. Annual growth, by contrast, is stationary. The piecewise growth model
selects $\NumRegimes$ broad regimes in the Maddison-derived annual GDP series
(Table~\ref{tab:regimes}), and a sequential parametric-bootstrap break test
agrees that additional regimes beyond this point are not statistically
supported. These regimes are descriptive statistical summaries; they are not
automatic causal explanations.

The expanded fiscal and distributional layers change the interpretation. Federal
debt, deficits, and tax laws help structure the historical narrative, but they
are endogenous to the same shocks that affect GDP. More importantly, GDP per
capita and real worker earnings diverged strongly after the index base year
$\IndexBaseYear$. Indexed to $\IndexBaseYear$, real GDP per capita was about
$\GdppcIndexLatest$ by $\DistLatestYear$ (more than double its base-year level),
while real median weekly earnings were about $\MedianEarningsIndexLatest$. In
other words, aggregate output per person more than doubled, while the median
weekly earnings index rose only modestly above its base.

\section{Data and Reproducibility}

\subsection{Core GDP Series}

The primary GDP series is built from the Maddison Project Database 2023 by
multiplying United States real GDP per capita by population. This produces a
long-run real GDP proxy suitable for growth-rate and log-trend analysis from
1920 onward. The modern validation source is FRED/BEA annual real GDP
(\texttt{GDPCA}), available from 1929 onward.

The source-validation table compares Maddison-derived growth with FRED/BEA
growth from $\OverlapStart$ to $\OverlapEnd$. The overlap has $\OverlapYears$
annual observations, a growth-rate correlation of $\GrowthCorrelation$, a mean
absolute difference of $\GrowthMad$ percentage points, and an RMSE of
$\GrowthRmse$ percentage points. This is close enough for regime-level analysis,
while still reminding the reader that Maddison historical estimates and BEA
national accounts are not the same object.

\subsection{Fiscal, Tax, and Distributional Data}

The fiscal context layer uses FRED/OMB federal debt, receipts, outlays, deficit,
and interest-outlay ratios as percent of GDP. The tax-regime event layer uses a
curated catalog of broad federal tax-law changes, with signed ordinal shock
values and narrative classifications. The distributional layer adds BEA real GDP
per capita, BLS real median weekly earnings, BLS real hourly compensation, BEA
federal receipt composition, IRS top and bottom statutory income-tax rates, and
BLS Consumer Expenditure federal income-tax rates by income quintile.

These series answer different questions. GDP per capita measures aggregate real
output per person. Median weekly earnings measure the real earnings of the
median full-time wage and salary worker. Federal income-tax rates by Consumer
Expenditure income quintile measure average federal income-tax burdens for
consumer units, not the full incidence of the tax-and-transfer system.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Trend Model and Unit-Root Diagnostics}

The long-run trend is estimated on log real GDP:

\begin{equation}
  \log(GDP_t) = \alpha + \beta \cdot year_t + \epsilon_t.
\end{equation}

The annualized trend growth rate is computed as $\exp(\beta)-1$. Because annual
macroeconomic series are highly autocorrelated, the slope is reported with
Newey-West heteroskedasticity- and autocorrelation-consistent (HAC) standard
errors \cite{neweywest1987}, using $\TrendNwLags$ lags on $\TrendNobs$
observations. To guard against a spurious-trend reading, the log level and the
growth series are each tested with the augmented Dickey-Fuller test
\cite{saiddickey1984}, whose null is a unit root, and the KPSS test
\cite{kpss1992}, whose null is stationarity. Reporting both avoids over-reading a
single test.

\subsection{Piecewise Growth Regimes and Break Inference}

Growth regimes are estimated on annual percent changes:

\begin{equation}
  growth_t = \mu_j + u_t,\quad t \in \text{regime } j.
\end{equation}

The algorithm searches over candidate breakpoints with dynamic programming and
selects the segmentation that minimizes a Gaussian information criterion (BIC by
default), counting each segment mean, each break location, and the common
residual variance as free parameters. A segment is labelled ``above mean'' if
its mean growth rate exceeds the full-sample mean. This is a descriptive label,
not a welfare or causality statement.

The number of regimes is not taken on faith from the criterion. Each additional
break is tested with a sequential $\text{supF}(l+1\,|\,l)$ statistic in the
spirit of Bai and Perron \cite{baiperron1998,baiperron2003}. Because the break
date is estimated, the statistic does not follow a standard $F$ distribution, so
the p-value is obtained by a parametric bootstrap ($\BreakTestBootstrap$
replications) that simulates data from the fitted smaller model. Uncertainty in
the break \emph{locations} is quantified separately by a residual bootstrap
($\BreakCiBootstrap$ replications) that re-segments each resample and reports
percentile intervals for every break year.

\subsection{Dynamic Tax Effects}

The dynamic tax-effects layer uses two complementary designs. First, local
projections \cite{jorda2005} estimate the association between a signed tax shock
and future GDP growth at each horizon:

\begin{equation}
  growth_{t+h} = \alpha_h + \beta_h shock_t +
  \gamma_h growth_{t-1} + \gamma'_h growth_{t-2} + e_{t+h}.
\end{equation}

Because the horizon-$h$ local-projection residual is serially correlated up to
order $h$ by construction, the Newey-West lag length grows with the horizon
rather than being fixed. Second, distributed-lag regressions include current and
lagged tax shocks in a single annual growth equation. Both designs estimate
delayed dynamics, but they do not solve identification by themselves. The report
therefore separates all catalogued tax events from a smaller subset classified
as plausibly exogenous long-run reforms.

\subsection{Distributional Analysis}

The distributional layer indexes real GDP per capita, real median weekly
earnings, and real hourly compensation to a common base year:

\begin{equation}
  index_{x,t} = 100 \times \frac{x_t}{x_{base}}.
\end{equation}

The main gap measure is:

\begin{equation}
  gap_t = index_{GDPpc,t} - index_{median earnings,t}.
\end{equation}

Tax-burden shift is approximated with three sets of proxies: receipt
composition, statutory top-bottom income-tax rate spread, and Consumer
Expenditure federal income-tax rates by income quintile. These proxies do not
constitute a full incidence model, but they directly address whether the tax
structure moved away from more progressive bases and whether this aligns with
GDP growth or worker earnings.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Long-Run GDP Trend and Growth Regimes}

Figure~\ref{fig:trend} shows the log real GDP path and fitted linear trend. The
in-sample fit is high ($R^2 = \TrendRSquared$) and the slope is precisely
estimated even under HAC standard errors (slope $t = \TrendHacT$). That
precision, however, is exactly what a near-integrated series produces: the
augmented Dickey-Fuller test does not reject a unit root in log real GDP (ADF
statistic $\LevelAdfStat$, $p \LevelAdfP$), and the KPSS test rejects
trend-stationarity ($p \LevelKpssP$). The annual growth series behaves the
opposite way --- ADF rejects a unit root ($p \GrowthAdfP$) and KPSS does not
reject stationarity ($p \GrowthKpssP$). This is the formal justification for
analysing regimes on growth rather than on the level.

\subsection{Fiscal Context}

Adding federal fiscal ratios changes the narrative from a pure growth story to a
macro-fiscal story. Debt and deficit measures vary sharply across wars,
recessions, and crisis periods. Same-year correlations between fiscal ratios and
GDP growth are modest. In the generated panel, gross debt-to-GDP has a same-year
correlation with GDP growth of about $\GrossDebtCorrSame$ and a one-year-lag
correlation of about $\GrossDebtCorrLagOne$. Receipts, outlays, and deficits have
smaller simple correlations with annual growth.

\subsection{Tax-Regime Dynamics}

The local-projection estimates in Figure~\ref{fig:taxlp} show delayed paths
after signed tax-regime shocks. Confidence intervals are wide, especially for
the plausibly exogenous subset, because there are few major long-run reform
events in the historical sample. The result should therefore be read as a
dynamic screening exercise rather than a definitive tax multiplier estimate.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{tax_local_projections.png}
  \caption{Local-projection estimates of GDP-growth associations after signed
  tax-regime shocks. The green line restricts the treatment series to events
  classified as plausibly exogenous long-run reforms.}
  \label{fig:taxlp}
\end{figure}

The most important methodological improvement over a same-year event study is
that the model allows effects to appear with delay. The data do not support a
simple one-year story. Some horizons are negative, some positive, and most
intervals overlap zero. That pattern is consistent with the fact that tax laws
include anticipation, phase-ins, temporary provisions, and heterogeneous
channels across households and firms.

\section{Tax Burden Shift and Real Wages}

\subsection{GDP Per Capita versus Worker Buying Power}

Figure~\ref{fig:wagegap} is central to the distributional interpretation. Using
$\IndexBaseYear$ as the common index base, real GDP per capita reached about
$\GdppcIndexLatest$ by $\DistLatestYear$, while real median weekly earnings
reached about $\MedianEarningsIndexLatest$. Real hourly compensation
($\HourlyCompIndexLatest$) rose more than median weekly earnings, but still much
less than GDP per capita. This means aggregate output per person can increase
while the median worker's measured real earnings grow slowly.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{wage_gdp_gap.png}
  \caption{Real GDP per capita, real median weekly earnings, and real hourly
  compensation indexed to $\IndexBaseYear$. The widening gap shows why GDP growth
  is not equivalent to broad worker buying-power growth.}
  \label{fig:wagegap}
\end{figure}

This does not mean most people necessarily became poorer in every year. It means
the aggregate growth measure and the median wage-worker measure diverged
substantially. A welfare analysis would also need hours worked, household
composition, transfers, taxes, fringe benefits, asset income, housing costs, and
consumption prices by income group.

\subsection{Tax Burden Shift Proxies}

Figure~\ref{fig:taxburden} combines three views of tax burden. The first panel
shows the composition of selected federal receipts. Social-insurance
contributions rose from about $\SocialInsuranceShareFirst\%$ of the selected
federal receipt base in $\ReceiptFirstYear$ to about
$\SocialInsuranceShareLatest\%$ in $\ReceiptLatestYear$. Corporate income taxes
fell from about $\CorporateShareFirst\%$ to about $\CorporateShareLatest\%$ over
the same endpoint comparison. This is consistent with a long-run movement away
from corporate income taxes and toward social-insurance financing.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{tax_burden_shift.png}
  \caption{Tax burden shift proxies. Receipt composition captures the rise of
  social-insurance contributions relative to corporate income taxes; quintile
  rates show average federal income-tax rates by income group where Consumer
  Expenditure data are available.}
  \label{fig:taxburden}
\end{figure}

The Consumer Expenditure quintile panel begins in $\QuintileFirstYear$. In that
year, the highest income quintile's federal income-tax rate exceeded the average
of the bottom four quintiles by about $\QuintileSpreadFirst$ percentage points.
By $\QuintileLatestYear$, that spread was about $\QuintileSpreadLatest$
percentage points. This particular income-tax measure therefore does not show a
simple shift of federal income-tax burden away from the top quintile. The broader
burden story is more nuanced: payroll-style financing became more important,
corporate income-tax receipts became less important, and income-tax
progressivity by quintile remained substantial.

\subsection{Association Screen}

The lagged association screen regresses GDP growth, real median weekly earnings
growth, and the GDP/wage gap on one-year-lagged burden-shift proxies. These are
single-predictor models with HAC standard errors. The strongest pattern in the
current output is not a robust GDP-growth claim; it is that the GDP/wage gap is
statistically associated with some tax progressivity proxies. For example, the
one-year-lag statutory top-bottom rate spread has a negative coefficient in the
GDP/wage-gap regression, while the quintile rate spread has a positive
coefficient. These associations should be interpreted cautiously because the
models are low-dimensional and do not control for technology, unionization,
trade exposure, monetary policy, inflation, demographic composition, or
sectoral change.

\section{Limitations}

The main limitation is identification. Breakpoints, fiscal ratios, tax laws, and
wages all move with macroeconomic conditions. Recessions change tax receipts and
policy decisions; wars change debt, output, and tax rates; inflation and
monetary policy affect both GDP and real earnings. The tax-shock catalog partly
addresses this by separating plausibly exogenous reforms from deficit, wartime,
and countercyclical changes, but the classification remains historical and
sample sizes are small.

The second limitation is measurement. Maddison is useful for long-run history
but is not identical to BEA national accounts. Median weekly earnings are not
the same as household income or total compensation. Consumer Expenditure
quintile tax rates cover federal income-tax amounts, not the full tax-and-
transfer system. Receipt composition is a proxy for burden shifting, not a
complete incidence analysis.

The third limitation is temporal coverage. The Maddison-based GDP regime sample
ends in $\PostwarEnd$, while several modern FRED series used in the fiscal and
distributional layers extend to $\DistLatestYear$ and may include an annualized
or partial latest-year observation. The report therefore reports explicit
coverage by series and avoids implying that every dataset covers the same
complete historical window.

\section{Conclusion}

The GDP regime analysis shows that the United States did not grow at one
constant annual speed after 1920. A log trend summarizes the long-run level
in-sample, but the level is statistically indistinguishable from a unit-root
process, so stationary annual growth --- read through a small number of
statistically supported regimes --- is the appropriate object of study. Fiscal
and tax data enrich the narrative, especially by showing how debt, deficits,
tax-law changes, and federal receipt composition line up with macroeconomic
periods.

The distributional extension changes the substantive interpretation. Real GDP
per capita and real median weekly earnings diverged strongly after
$\IndexBaseYear$. That does not negate GDP growth; it clarifies who may have
participated in it. From a data-science perspective, the correct conclusion is
not that GDP is useless, but that GDP is incomplete. A credible assessment of
economic regimes should report aggregate output, fiscal structure, tax burden
proxies, and worker purchasing power together.
