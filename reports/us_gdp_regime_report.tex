\begin{abstract}
This report summarizes a reproducible empirical workflow for long-run United
States real GDP growth from 1920 onward. The analysis combines a Maddison-based
real GDP proxy, FRED/BEA validation data, piecewise growth-regime detection,
federal fiscal context, tax-regime event dynamics, and distributional measures
linking GDP per capita to real worker earnings. The headline statistical result
is that a single long-run trend fits the log GDP level well, but annual growth is
better read as a sequence of regimes. The distributional result is equally
important: aggregate real GDP per capita rose much faster than real median
weekly earnings after the late 1970s, so GDP growth alone is an incomplete proxy
for broad buying power. Fiscal and tax variables add useful context, but the
report distinguishes descriptive timing, lagged association, and causal
identification throughout.
\end{abstract}

\keywords{United States GDP, growth regimes, public debt, tax policy, real wages,
distributional analysis, local projections, reproducible research}

\section{Executive Summary}

The project began with a simple question: whether the United States grew at one
roughly constant speed after 1920, or whether the data support distinct growth
regimes. The answer is mixed in the way macroeconomic time series usually are:
the log level of real GDP has a strong long-run trend, but the annual growth
process is segmented by depression, war, demobilisation, and a long postwar
period.

The fitted log-trend model implies an annualized trend growth rate of
approximately $3.16\%$ and an $R^2$ of $0.987$. The piecewise growth model
selects five broad regimes in the Maddison-derived annual GDP series: 1921--1928,
1929--1933, 1934--1944, 1945--1949, and 1950--2022. These regimes are
descriptive statistical summaries; they are not automatic causal explanations.

The expanded fiscal and distributional layers change the interpretation. Federal
debt, deficits, and tax laws help structure the historical narrative, but they
are endogenous to the same shocks that affect GDP. More importantly, GDP per
capita and real worker earnings diverged strongly after 1979. Indexed to 1979,
real GDP per capita was about $208.6$ in 2023, while real median weekly earnings
were about $110.4$. In other words, aggregate output per person more than
doubled, while the median weekly earnings index increased by only about
$10.4\%$ over the same base.

\section{Data and Reproducibility}

\subsection{Core GDP Series}

The primary GDP series is built from the Maddison Project Database 2023 by
multiplying United States real GDP per capita by population. This produces a
long-run real GDP proxy suitable for growth-rate and log-trend analysis from
1920 onward. The modern validation source is FRED/BEA annual real GDP
(\texttt{GDPCA}), available from 1929 onward.

The source-validation table compares Maddison-derived growth with FRED/BEA
growth from 1930 to 2022. The overlap has 93 annual observations, a growth-rate
correlation of $0.955$, a mean absolute difference of $0.626$ percentage points,
and an RMSE of $1.471$ percentage points. This is close enough for regime-level
analysis, while still reminding the reader that Maddison historical estimates
and BEA national accounts are not the same object.

\subsection{Fiscal, Tax, and Distributional Data}

The fiscal context layer uses FRED/OMB federal debt, receipts, outlays, deficit,
and interest-outlay ratios as percent of GDP. The tax-regime event layer uses a
curated catalog of broad federal tax-law changes, with signed ordinal shock
values and narrative classifications. The distributional layer adds BEA real GDP
per capita, BLS real median weekly earnings, BLS real hourly compensation, BEA
federal receipt composition, IRS top and bottom statutory income-tax rates, and
BLS Consumer Expenditure federal income-tax rates by income quintile.

These series answer different questions. GDP per capita measures aggregate real
output per person. Median weekly earnings measure the real earnings of the
median full-time wage and salary worker. Federal income-tax rates by Consumer
Expenditure income quintile measure average federal income-tax burdens for
consumer units, not the full incidence of the tax-and-transfer system.

\section{Methods}

\subsection{Trend Model}

The long-run trend is estimated on log real GDP:

\begin{equation}
  \log(GDP_t) = \alpha + \beta \cdot year_t + \epsilon_t.
\end{equation}

The annualized trend growth rate is computed as $\exp(\beta)-1$. This captures
average exponential growth, but it intentionally does not model nonlinear
business-cycle dynamics.

\subsection{Piecewise Growth Regimes}

Growth regimes are estimated on annual percent changes:

\begin{equation}
  growth_t = \mu_j + u_t,\quad t \in \text{regime } j.
\end{equation}

The algorithm searches over candidate breakpoints with dynamic programming and
selects the segmentation with a BIC-style penalty. A segment is labelled
``above mean'' if its mean growth rate exceeds the full-sample mean. This is a
descriptive label, not a welfare or causality statement.

\subsection{Dynamic Tax Effects}

The dynamic tax-effects layer uses two complementary designs. First, local
projections estimate the association between a signed tax shock and future GDP
growth at each horizon:

\begin{equation}
  growth_{t+h} = \alpha_h + \beta_h shock_t +
  \gamma_h growth_{t-1} + \gamma'_h growth_{t-2} + e_{t+h}.
\end{equation}

Second, distributed-lag regressions include current and lagged tax shocks in a
single annual growth equation. Both designs estimate delayed dynamics, but they
do not solve identification by themselves. The report therefore separates all
catalogued tax events from a smaller subset classified as plausibly exogenous
long-run reforms.

\subsection{Distributional Analysis}

The distributional layer indexes real GDP per capita, real median weekly
earnings, and real hourly compensation to a common base year:

\begin{equation}
  index_{x,t} = 100 \times \frac{x_t}{x_{base}}.
\end{equation}

The main gap measure is:

\begin{equation}
  gap_t = index_{GDPpc,t} - index_{median earnings,t}.
\end{equation}

Tax-burden shift is approximated with three sets of proxies: receipt
composition, statutory top-bottom income-tax rate spread, and Consumer
Expenditure federal income-tax rates by income quintile. These proxies do not
constitute a full incidence model, but they directly address whether the tax
structure moved away from more progressive bases and whether this aligns with
GDP growth or worker earnings.

\section{Results}

\subsection{Long-Run GDP Trend and Growth Regimes}

Figure~\ref{fig:trend} shows the log real GDP path and fitted linear trend.
The model fit is high, but the residuals and annual growth series show why the
trend alone is not an adequate historical summary.

\subsection{Fiscal Context}

Adding federal fiscal ratios changes the narrative from a pure growth story to a
macro-fiscal story. Debt and deficit measures vary sharply across wars,
recessions, and crisis periods. Same-year correlations between fiscal ratios and
GDP growth are modest. In the generated panel, gross debt-to-GDP has a
same-year correlation with GDP growth of about $-0.317$ and a one-year-lag
correlation of about $-0.261$. Receipts, outlays, and deficits have smaller
simple correlations with annual growth.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{fiscal_context.png}
  \caption{Federal debt, budget ratios, and annual GDP growth. The figure is a
  timing and context device; it does not identify fiscal causality.}
  \label{fig:fiscal}
\end{figure}

\subsection{Tax-Regime Dynamics}

The local-projection estimates in Figure~\ref{fig:taxlp} show delayed paths
after signed tax-regime shocks. Confidence intervals are wide, especially for
the plausibly exogenous subset, because there are few major long-run reform
events in the historical sample. The result should therefore be read as a
dynamic screening exercise rather than a definitive tax multiplier estimate.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{tax_local_projections.png}
  \caption{Local-projection estimates of GDP-growth associations after signed
  tax-regime shocks. The green line restricts the treatment series to events
  classified as plausibly exogenous long-run reforms.}
  \label{fig:taxlp}
\end{figure}

The most important methodological improvement over a same-year event study is
that the model allows effects to appear with delay. The data do not support a
simple one-year story. Some horizons are negative, some positive, and most
intervals overlap zero. That pattern is consistent with the fact that tax laws
include anticipation, phase-ins, temporary provisions, and heterogeneous
channels across households and firms.

\section{Tax Burden Shift and Real Wages}

\subsection{GDP Per Capita versus Worker Buying Power}

Figure~\ref{fig:wagegap} is central to the distributional interpretation. Using
1979 as the common index base, real GDP per capita reached about $208.6$ by
2023, while real median weekly earnings reached about $110.4$. Real hourly
compensation rose more than median weekly earnings, but still much less than
GDP per capita. This means aggregate output per person can increase while the
median worker's measured real earnings grow slowly.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{wage_gdp_gap.png}
  \caption{Real GDP per capita, real median weekly earnings, and real hourly
  compensation indexed to 1979. The widening gap shows why GDP growth is not
  equivalent to broad worker buying-power growth.}
  \label{fig:wagegap}
\end{figure}

This does not mean most people necessarily became poorer in every year. It means
the aggregate growth measure and the median wage-worker measure diverged
substantially. A welfare analysis would also need hours worked, household
composition, transfers, taxes, fringe benefits, asset income, housing costs, and
consumption prices by income group.

\subsection{Tax Burden Shift Proxies}

Figure~\ref{fig:taxburden} combines three views of tax burden. The first panel
shows the composition of selected federal receipts. Social-insurance
contributions rose from about $15.1\%$ of the selected federal receipt base in
1947 to about $35.4\%$ in 2023. Corporate income taxes fell from about $29.6\%$
to about $8.4\%$ over the same endpoint comparison. This is consistent with a
long-run movement away from corporate income taxes and toward social-insurance
financing.

\begin{figure}[H]
  \centering
  \includegraphics[width=\linewidth]{tax_burden_shift.png}
  \caption{Tax burden shift proxies. Receipt composition captures the rise of
  social-insurance contributions relative to corporate income taxes; quintile
  rates show average federal income-tax rates by income group where Consumer
  Expenditure data are available.}
  \label{fig:taxburden}
\end{figure}

The Consumer Expenditure quintile panel begins in 1984. In that year, the
highest income quintile's federal income-tax rate exceeded the average of the
bottom four quintiles by about $5.25$ percentage points. By 2023, that spread
was about $14.10$ percentage points. This particular income-tax measure therefore
does not show a simple shift of federal income-tax burden away from the top
quintile. The broader burden story is more nuanced: payroll-style financing
became more important, corporate income-tax receipts became less important, and
income-tax progressivity by quintile remained substantial.

\subsection{Association Screen}

The lagged association screen regresses GDP growth, real median weekly earnings
growth, and the GDP/wage gap on one-year-lagged burden-shift proxies. These are
single-predictor models with HAC standard errors. The strongest pattern in the
current output is not a robust GDP-growth claim; it is that the GDP/wage gap is
statistically associated with some tax progressivity proxies. For example, the
one-year-lag statutory top-bottom rate spread has a negative coefficient in the
GDP/wage-gap regression, while the quintile rate spread has a positive
coefficient. These associations should be interpreted cautiously because the
models are low-dimensional and do not control for technology, unionization,
trade exposure, monetary policy, inflation, demographic composition, or
sectoral change.

\section{Limitations}

The main limitation is identification. Breakpoints, fiscal ratios, tax laws, and
wages all move with macroeconomic conditions. Recessions change tax receipts and
policy decisions; wars change debt, output, and tax rates; inflation and
monetary policy affect both GDP and real earnings. The tax-shock catalog partly
addresses this by separating plausibly exogenous reforms from deficit,
wartime, and countercyclical changes, but the classification remains historical
and sample sizes are small.

The second limitation is measurement. Maddison is useful for long-run history
but is not identical to BEA national accounts. Median weekly earnings are not
the same as household income or total compensation. Consumer Expenditure
quintile tax rates cover federal income-tax amounts, not the full tax-and-
transfer system. Receipt composition is a proxy for burden shifting, not a
complete incidence analysis.

The third limitation is temporal coverage. Some modern FRED series include
annualized or partial latest-year observations, while Consumer Expenditure
quintile data currently end earlier. The report therefore reports explicit
coverage by series and avoids implying that every dataset covers the same
complete historical window.

\section{Conclusion}

The GDP regime analysis shows that the United States did not grow at one
constant annual speed after 1920. A log trend summarizes the long-run level
well, but annual growth is better read through regimes. Fiscal and tax data
enrich the narrative, especially by showing how debt, deficits, tax-law changes,
and federal receipt composition line up with macroeconomic periods.

The distributional extension changes the substantive interpretation. Real GDP
per capita and real median weekly earnings diverged strongly after 1979. That
does not negate GDP growth; it clarifies who may have participated in it. From a
data-science perspective, the correct conclusion is not that GDP is useless, but
that GDP is incomplete. A credible assessment of economic regimes should report
aggregate output, fiscal structure, tax burden proxies, and worker purchasing
power together.
